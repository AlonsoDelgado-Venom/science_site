About Me

I am an evolutionary biologist and Postdoc at Harvard University in the Department of Organismic and Evolutionary Biology. I received my Ph.D. from The Ohio State University. My work integrates genomics, morphology, and field expeditions to decode the origin of complex traits.

Beyond the science, I am a first-generation Mexican American dedicated to building equitable infrastructure in STEM through systemic mentorship and community leadership.

Current Research: Selection, Diversity & Innovation

Drivers of Venom Evolution

Venom is one of the most dynamic traits in the animal kingdom. My research explores how natural selection shapes venom composition across vast evolutionary timescales. By investigating the species diversity of sea anemones and cephalopods, I seek to identify the ecological and genomic drivers that trigger the emergence of novel toxins.

Evolutionary Organogenesis

Building on these evolutionary patterns, I examine the physical and molecular mechanisms of organogenesis. Using cephalopods and ants as natural experiments, I examine how changes in gland morphology and tissue organization influence modern venom gene expression.
